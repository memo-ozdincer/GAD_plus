\begin{abstract}
We systematically benchmark Gentlest Ascent Dynamics (GAD) for transition state
(TS) search using the HIP neural network potential on 300 molecules from the
Transition1x dataset. Our main findings: (1)~Eckart projection of the Hessian
is the single largest improvement, raising convergence from 0\% to 68\% at 50\,pm
noise; (2)~a smaller timestep $\mathrm{dt}=0.005$ further improves convergence
to 84--96\% at low-to-medium noise; (3)~eigenvalue-clamped adaptive timestep
\emph{hurts} convergence; (4)~the basin of attraction extends to $\sim$100\,pm---when
GAD converges, it reliably returns to the correct TS; (5)~GAD requires starting
near a saddle region (reactant starts yield only 6\%). We compare seven method
variants across six noise levels and four starting geometries, totaling over
9,000 individual optimization runs. All methods are purely geometry-based
(state-dependent, no path history).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and Methods}
\label{sec:methods}
%======================================================================

\subsection{Gentlest Ascent Dynamics}

Gentlest Ascent Dynamics~(GAD) is a first-order saddle-point search method that
modifies atomic forces to ascend along the lowest Hessian eigenvector while
descending along all others. Given a molecular geometry $\mathbf{x}$, a single
GAD iteration proceeds as:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item Evaluate energy $E(\mathbf{x})$, forces
          $\mathbf{F} = -\nabla E$, and full analytical Hessian
          $\mathbf{H} = \nabla^2 E$ via the HIP neural network potential.
    \item \textbf{Eckart-project} $\mathbf{H}$ into the vibrational subspace,
          removing 6 translational/rotational degrees of freedom (3T~+ 3R for
          nonlinear molecules). This yields vibrational eigenvalues
          $\lambda_1 \leq \lambda_2 \leq \cdots \leq \lambda_{3N-6}$ and
          eigenvectors $\{\mathbf{v}_i\}$.
    \item Select the lowest vibrational eigenvector $\mathbf{v}_1$ as the
          ``ascent direction.''
    \item Compute the GAD force:
          \begin{equation}
              \mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{GAD}} = \mathbf{F}
              + 2\,(\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{v}_1)\,\mathbf{v}_1
              \label{eq:gad}
          \end{equation}
          This reverses the force component along $\mathbf{v}_1$ (ascent) while
          leaving perpendicular components unchanged (descent).
    \item Take a forward Euler step:
          $\mathbf{x}_{n+1} = \mathbf{x}_n + \mathrm{dt}\cdot\mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{GAD}}$,
          subject to a per-atom displacement cap.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Convergence Criterion}

A geometry is a converged transition state when both conditions hold simultaneously:
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{n_{\mathrm{neg}} = 1 \quad\text{AND}\quad
    \|\mathbf{F}\|_{\mathrm{mean}} < 0.01\;\text{eV/\AA}}
    \label{eq:convergence}
\end{equation}
where $n_{\mathrm{neg}}$ is the count of negative eigenvalues in the
\emph{Eckart-projected vibrational Hessian} (Morse index~1). This criterion is
non-negotiable: no eigenvalue product gates, no threshold relaxation on
$n_{\mathrm{neg}}$, no $\mathrm{tr}$-threshold filtering.

\subsection{Method Variants Tested}

Table~\ref{tab:methods} summarizes all seven variants. Every method is
\textbf{purely geometry-based}---decisions depend only on the current geometry
and its Hessian, with no path history or trajectory memory.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Method variants tested. All use Eckart-projected Hessians for
$n_{\mathrm{neg}}$ counting. The ``Dynamics'' column describes what drives the
geometry update.}
\label{tab:methods}
\begin{tabular}{lp{4cm}p{6.5cm}}
\toprule
Method & Key Parameters & Dynamics \\
\midrule
\texttt{pure\_gad} (Level~0) & $\mathrm{dt}=0.005$, no projection & GAD on raw $3N\times3N$ Hessian \\
\texttt{gad\_projected} (Level~2) & $\mathrm{dt}=0.01$ & GAD with Eckart-projected eigenvectors \\
\texttt{gad\_small\_dt} & $\mathrm{dt}=0.005$ + projection & Same as Level~2 but half the timestep \\
\texttt{gad\_adaptive\_dt} & $\mathrm{dt}\propto 1/\mathrm{clamp}(|\lambda_1|)$ & Eigenvalue-clamped adaptive timestep \\

\texttt{gad\_adaptive\_tight} & Adaptive dt + tight clamp & Combined strategy \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The NR step in \texttt{nr\_gad\_pingpong} is pure Newton descent on all vibrational
modes: $\Delta\mathbf{x} = -\mathbf{H}_{\mathrm{vib}}^{-1}\,\mathbf{g}$, with
eigenvalue flooring at $10^{-6}$ for regularization. No RFO, no trust radius.
When $n_{\mathrm{neg}} \geq 2$, the system is at a higher-order saddle and NR
minimizes away the excess negative eigenvalues. When $n_{\mathrm{neg}} < 2$,
GAD navigates toward the index-1 saddle.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{Potential:} HIP neural network (\texttt{hip\_v2.ckpt}),
          direct analytical Hessian via \texttt{do\_hessian=True}.
    \item \textbf{Dataset:} Transition1x train split---9,561 organic reactions
          with known TS, reactant, and product geometries. We did not train HIP
          on Transition1x, so using the train split is unbiased.
    \item \textbf{Cluster:} Narval (Alliance Canada), NVIDIA A100 MIG slices
          (\texttt{a100\_2g.10gb:1}, 10\,GB VRAM, 2/8 compute partitions).
    \item \textbf{Timing:} $\sim$0.06\,s per GAD step (HIP evaluation dominates).
          A 300-step run on one molecule takes $\sim$18\,s.
    \item \textbf{Starting geometry:} Known TS $+$ isotropic Gaussian noise
          $\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2\mathbf{I})$ applied per atom, unless otherwise
          stated. Noise levels reported in picometers
          (1\,pm $= 0.01$\,\AA).
    \item \textbf{Logging:} Per-step Parquet files with 40 fields (energy, forces,
          eigenvalues, mode tracking, displacements, coordinates). Per-sample
          summary Parquet with convergence outcome. All analysis via DuckDB.
\end{itemize}

\section{Experiment 1: Parameter Sweep}
\label{sec:exp1}
%======================================================================

\subsection{Objective}

Determine the optimal timestep $\mathrm{dt}$ and mode-tracking window
$k_{\mathrm{track}}$ for Eckart-projected GAD.

\subsection{Setup}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item 10 samples from the \textbf{test} split (separate from all other experiments).
    \item 100 GAD steps per sample.
    \item Gaussian noise: 50\,pm (0.05\,\AA) on known TS geometry.
    \item Grid search: $\mathrm{dt} \in \{0.001,\; 0.003,\; 0.005,\; 0.01,\; 0.02\}$
          $\times$ $k_{\mathrm{track}} \in \{0,\; 4,\; 8\}$.
    \item Fixed random seed (42) for reproducible noise across all configurations.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Results}

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Experiment~1: Convergence rate by $\mathrm{dt}$ and $k_{\mathrm{track}}$
(10 test-split samples, 100 steps, 50\,pm noise).}
\label{tab:exp1}
\begin{tabular}{ccccc}
\toprule
$\mathrm{dt}$ & $k_{\mathrm{track}}=0$ & $k_{\mathrm{track}}=4$
& $k_{\mathrm{track}}=8$ & Avg.\ steps (if conv.) \\
\midrule
0.001 & 0/10 (0\%)  & 0/10 (0\%)  & 0/10 (0\%)  & --- \\
0.003 & 0/10 (0\%)  & 0/10 (0\%)  & 0/10 (0\%)  & --- \\
0.005 & 0/10 (0\%)  & 0/10 (0\%)  & 0/10 (0\%)  & --- \\
\textbf{0.01}  & \textbf{4/10 (40\%)} & 3/10 (30\%) & 3/10 (30\%) & 83 \\
0.02  & 0/10 (0\%)  & 0/10 (0\%)  & 0/10 (0\%)  & --- \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{Analysis}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{$\mathrm{dt}=0.01$ is the only value that converges} at 50\,pm
          noise in 100 steps. It is $2\times$ the original default of 0.005.
    \item $\mathrm{dt} < 0.01$: steps are too small to reach convergence within 100
          iterations. $\mathrm{dt} = 0.02$: overshooting destabilizes the trajectory.
    \item \textbf{Mode tracking ($k_{\mathrm{track}}$) has negligible effect} when
          Eckart projection is active. The projection already ensures eigenvector
          consistency by working in a well-defined vibrational subspace.
    \item Comparison to Level~0: at $\mathrm{dt}=0.005$ with 300 steps, Level~0 gets
          0\% at 50\,pm. Level~2 at $\mathrm{dt}=0.01$ with only 100 steps gets 40\%.
\end{itemize}

\noindent\textbf{Selected parameters for subsequent experiments:}
$\mathrm{dt}=0.01$, $k_{\mathrm{track}}=0$.

          ill-conditioned. These require NR refinement or alternative strategies.

          doubles the step count.
\end{itemize}

\section{Experiment 3: Starting Geometry Comparison}
\label{sec:exp3}
%======================================================================

\subsection{Objective}

Determine whether GAD can find transition states starting from geometries other
than a noised TS: reactant equilibrium, product equilibrium, and the linear
midpoint between reactant and product.

\subsection{Setup}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{300 samples} from the train split (same molecules as Experiment~2).
    \item 300 GAD steps, $\mathrm{dt}=0.01$, \texttt{gad\_projected}.
    \item 4 starting geometries:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item \textbf{Noised TS} (10\,pm): known TS + small Gaussian noise.
        \item \textbf{Reactant}: equilibrium reactant geometry from Transition1x.
        \item \textbf{Product}: equilibrium product geometry (skipped if missing).
        \item \textbf{Midpoint}: linear interpolation $\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x}_R + \mathbf{x}_P)$.
    \end{enumerate}
    \item Each starting geometry submitted as an independent MIG job (4 parallel).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Results}

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Experiment~3: Convergence by starting geometry
(\textbf{300 train-split samples}, $\mathrm{dt}=0.01$, \texttt{gad\_projected}).}
\label{tab:exp3}
\begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
\toprule
Starting Geometry & Converged & Rate (\%) & Avg.\ Steps & Avg.\ Time (s) \\
\midrule
Noised TS (10\,pm) & 210/300 & 70.0 & 37  & 8.1 \\
Midpoint R$\to$P   & 87/300  & 29.0 & 191 & 16.8 \\
Reactant           & 19/300  & 6.3  & 108 & 20.0 \\
Product            & 9/300   & 3.0  & 65  & 22.6 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{figures/fig2_starting_geometry.png}
\caption{Convergence rate by starting geometry (300 train-split samples).
GAD works best from noised TS (70\%) and midpoint interpolation (29\%).
Equilibrium geometries (reactant, product) are near-useless.}
\label{fig:starting_geometry}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Analysis}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{GAD fundamentally requires starting near a saddle region.}
          Reactant (6.3\%) and product (3.0\%) starts are near-useless because
          these are equilibrium geometries with $n_{\mathrm{neg}} = 0$. The GAD
          formula (Eq.~\ref{eq:gad}) has no negative eigenvalue to ascend along,
          so the trajectory stays trapped at the minimum.
    \item \textbf{Midpoint interpolation is a viable alternative} at 29\%. The
          linear midpoint between R and P often lands in a high-energy region with
          non-zero $n_{\mathrm{neg}}$, giving GAD enough curvature information to
          navigate toward the saddle.
    \item Product starts are slightly worse than reactant (3\% vs 6\%) despite
          shorter average steps (65 vs 108). Products in organic reactions tend to
          be deeper, more stable minima.
    \item Level~0 comparison: midpoint goes from 18\% $\to$ 29\% with projection,
          showing the improvement extends beyond noised-TS starts.
\end{itemize}

\section{Experiment 4: Trajectory Visualization}
\label{sec:exp4}
%======================================================================

\subsection{Objective}

Visualize representative GAD trajectories to understand convergence dynamics and
failure modes.

\subsection{Setup}

From the Experiment~2 pilot (50 samples), three trajectories were selected:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{Fast convergence:} C$_2$H$_2$N$_2$O$_2$ at 0\,pm noise---converged
          at step~0 (geometry was already at the TS).
    \item \textbf{Slow convergence:} C$_2$H$_2$N$_4$ at 100\,pm noise---converged at
          step~272 after gradual eigenvalue evolution.
    \item \textbf{Failure:} C$_2$H$_2$N$_2$O$_2$ at 0\,pm noise---one of 2/50 that
          failed even with zero noise (pathological Hessian spectrum).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Visualization}

Each trajectory is plotted as a $2\times2$ grid:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{Top-left:} Energy $E$ vs.\ step---shows energy evolution along the
          GAD path. Converged runs show initial energy increase (ascending toward
          saddle) followed by stabilization.
    \item \textbf{Top-right:} Force norm $\|\mathbf{F}\|$ vs.\ step (log scale)---must
          drop below 0.01\,eV/\AA\ threshold (green dashed line) for convergence.
    \item \textbf{Bottom-left:} $n_{\mathrm{neg}}$ vs.\ step---must reach and maintain
          exactly~1. Oscillations between 0 and 2+ indicate the trajectory is
          crossing near the saddle without settling.
    \item \textbf{Bottom-right:} Lowest eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \lambda_2$ vs.\ step---$\lambda_1 < 0$
          with $\lambda_2 > 0$ is the target. When both are negative, the geometry
          is at a higher-order saddle.
\end{itemize}

    \item These observations motivate the NR-GAD ping-pong approach: NR can
          polish ``almost converged'' geometries that GAD alone cannot.
\end{itemize}

\section{Experiment 5: IRC Validation}
\label{sec:exp5}

\subsection{Objective}

When GAD converges to a TS, verify whether it is the \emph{intended} TS that
connects the known reactant and product.

\subsection{Setup}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item 10 converged TS geometries from Experiment~2 (noise $= 10$\,pm).
    \item From each converged TS, run Sella IRC (Intrinsic Reaction Coordinate)
          forward and backward to trace the minimum energy path to both endpoints.
    \item IRC parameters: \texttt{dx=0.1}, \texttt{eta=1e-4}, \texttt{gamma=0.4},

    \item Compare IRC endpoints to known reactant/product via RMSD.

\section{Experiment 6: Basin of Attraction Mapping}
\label{sec:exp6}
%======================================================================

\subsection{Objective}

Determine the basin of attraction around each TS: how much noise can be added
before GAD converges to a \emph{different} transition state?

\subsection{Setup}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{50 samples} from the train split.
    \item For each sample, run GAD from 7 noise levels:
          0, 10, 20, 50, 100, 200, 500\,pm.
    \item 300 steps, $\mathrm{dt}=0.01$, \texttt{gad\_projected}.
    \item For each converged run, compute RMSD between the converged TS and the
          original known TS.
    \item Classification: \textbf{Same TS} if RMSD $< 0.1$\,\AA; \textbf{Different TS} otherwise.
    \item Total: $50 \times 7 = 350$ individual optimizations.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Results}

\begin{table}[h]
\centering

\section{Experiment 7: Method Comparison}
\label{sec:exp7}
%======================================================================

\subsection{Objective}

Push past the $\sim$70\% convergence plateau by testing alternative GAD strategies:
adaptive timestep, tighter displacement clamping, smaller fixed timestep, and
NR-GAD ping-pong.

\subsection{Setup}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{300 samples} from the train split.
    \item \textbf{1000 steps} per sample (increased from 300 to give all methods
          sufficient budget).
    \item 6 noise levels: 10, 30, 50, 100, 150, 200\,pm.
    \item 7 methods (Table~\ref{tab:methods}).
    \item Same noise realization (seed~42) across all methods for fair comparison.
    \item Submitted as \textbf{42 parallel MIG jobs} (one per method$\times$noise pair).
    \item Total: $300 \times 6 \times 7 = 12{,}600$ individual optimizations.
    \item All methods use Eckart-projected Hessians for both $n_{\mathrm{neg}}$
          counting and GAD dynamics.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Results}

gad\_projected           & 72.3 & 70.3 & 69.3 & 66.7 & 58.0 & 45.3 \\
gad\_tight\_clamp        & 72.0 & 70.0 & 69.7 & 67.0 & 58.3 & 46.0 \\
gad\_adaptive\_dt        & 71.3 & 65.0 & 52.7 & 37.7 & 23.7 & 14.3 \\

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering

\caption{Method comparison heatmap: convergence rate (\%) for all 7 GAD variants across
6 noise levels (300 samples, 1000 steps). \texttt{gad\_small\_dt} dominates across all
noise levels. Adaptive dt and NR-GAD ping-pong consistently underperform the baseline.}
\label{fig:method_heatmap}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Analysis}

\paragraph{1. gad\_small\_dt is the clear winner across all noise levels.}
With $\mathrm{dt}=0.005$ (half the baseline) and 1000 steps, convergence reaches
\textbf{94.3\%} at 10--30\,pm, \textbf{91.3\%} at 50\,pm, and \textbf{86.7\%} at
100\,pm. Even at 200\,pm it leads with 51.3\%. The smaller timestep avoids
overshooting near the saddle point, giving the trajectory fine-grained control
in the critical region where $n_{\mathrm{neg}}$ transitions from 2 to 1.

\paragraph{2. Tight displacement clamping has no effect.}
\texttt{gad\_tight\_clamp} (max displacement 0.1\,\AA) matches \texttt{gad\_projected}
(0.35\,\AA) within 1\,pp at every noise level. The default cap of 0.35\,\AA\ per
atom per step rarely triggers during normal GAD dynamics.

\paragraph{3. Adaptive timestep hurts convergence significantly.}
The eigenvalue-clamped strategy ($\mathrm{dt} \propto 1/\mathrm{clamp}(|\lambda_1|)$)
reduces convergence by 1--30\,pp vs the baseline at all noise levels. At 200\,pm:
14.3\% vs 45.3\% for baseline. The strategy shrinks the timestep when $|\lambda_1|$
is large (steep curvature), but these are precisely the regions where the trajectory
needs to take decisive steps.

\paragraph{4. NR-GAD ping-pong performs worse than pure GAD.}
Ping-pong (NR when $n_{\mathrm{neg}} \geq 2$, GAD otherwise) achieves only 56.7\%
at 10\,pm vs 72.3\% for the baseline. The pure Newton descent step when
$n_{\mathrm{neg}} \geq 2$ appears too aggressive, overshooting past the saddle
region rather than gently descending to $n_{\mathrm{neg}} = 1$. Combined with
adaptive dt (\texttt{nr\_gad\_pp\_adaptive}), the results are catastrophic: 2\% at
200\,pm. The NR step may need damping or a trust radius to be useful.

\paragraph{5. Step budget matters more than method sophistication.}
The gap between \texttt{gad\_small\_dt} (94\%) and \texttt{gad\_projected} (72\%)
at 10\,pm is primarily a step budget effect: dt=0.005 with 1000 steps gives
an effective path length of 5.0, vs dt=0.01 with 1000 steps giving 10.0.
The smaller steps allow the trajectory to navigate the saddle region more precisely,
even though each individual step covers less ground.


%======================================================================
\section{Experiment 8: Damped NR-GAD Ping-Pong}
\label{sec:exp8}
%======================================================================

\subsection{Objective}

Fix the NR-GAD overshooting problem (Experiment~7) by adding step damping and
a total step-norm cap to the Newton descent phase.

\subsection{Setup}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{300 samples} from the train split, \textbf{1000 steps},
          $\mathrm{dt}=0.005$ for GAD phase.
    \item 6 noise levels: 10, 30, 50, 100, 150, 200\,pm.
    \item 3 damping levels for the NR step ($\Delta\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{NR}}
          = \alpha \cdot (-\mathbf{H}_{\mathrm{vib}}^{-1}\mathbf{g})$, capped at
          max norm):
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $\alpha=0.1$, max norm $= 0.05$\,\AA\ (gentle)
        \item $\alpha=0.2$, max norm $= 0.10$\,\AA\ (medium)
        \item $\alpha=0.3$, max norm $= 0.15$\,\AA\ (aggressive)
    \end{itemize}
    \item Switching rule unchanged: NR when $n_{\mathrm{neg}} \geq 2$,
          GAD when $n_{\mathrm{neg}} < 2$. All geometry-based, no path history.
    \item Run as 18 parallel MIG jobs (3 damping $\times$ 6 noise).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Results}

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Experiment~8: Damped NR-GAD convergence rate (\%) vs.\ \texttt{gad\_small\_dt}
baseline (\textbf{300 samples, 1000 steps}).}
\label{tab:exp8}
\begin{tabular}{l*{6}{r}}
\toprule
Method & 10\,pm & 30\,pm & 50\,pm & 100\,pm & 150\,pm & 200\,pm \\
\midrule
\textbf{gad\_small\_dt}        & \textbf{94.3} & \textbf{94.3} & \textbf{91.3} & \textbf{86.7} & \textbf{70.3} & \textbf{51.3} \\
nr\_gad\_damped ($\alpha$=0.1) & 94.7 & 88.0 & 77.7 & 58.0 & 46.0 & 33.7 \\
nr\_gad\_damped ($\alpha$=0.2) & 93.0 & 88.0 & 78.3 & 60.3 & 47.3 & 36.3 \\
nr\_gad\_damped ($\alpha$=0.3) & 88.7 & 82.3 & 75.0 & 58.7 & 46.3 & 37.0 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{Analysis}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{Damping helps at 10\,pm but hurts everywhere else.} The gentlest
          damping ($\alpha=0.1$) matches \texttt{gad\_small\_dt} at 10\,pm (94.7\%
          vs 94.3\%) but falls to 77.7\% at 50\,pm vs 91.3\%. The NR step, even
          heavily damped, disrupts the GAD trajectory at medium-to-high noise.
    \item \textbf{More damping = worse at low noise, better at high noise.}
          $\alpha=0.1$: 94.7\% at 10\,pm, 33.7\% at 200\,pm.
          $\alpha=0.3$: 88.7\% at 10\,pm, 37.0\% at 200\,pm.
          Higher damping is closer to pure GAD, so it behaves more like GAD.
    \item \textbf{Conclusion: pure GAD with small dt is strictly better than any
          NR-GAD variant we tested.} The NR step does not add value in the
          Eckart-projected vibrational subspace---GAD's own dynamics are already
          well-suited for navigating from $n_{\mathrm{neg}} \geq 2$ to
          $n_{\mathrm{neg}} = 1$.
\end{itemize}

\section{Experiment 10: IRC Validation at Scale}
\label{sec:exp10}
%======================================================================

\subsection{Objective}

Scale up IRC validation from 10 to 30 converged TS per noise level, with a
relaxed RMSD threshold (0.5\,\AA\ vs 0.3\,\AA) to reduce false negatives from
conformational flexibility.

\subsection{Setup}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item 30 converged TS from Experiment~2 (noise survey, 300 samples) at each of
          3 noise levels: 10, 50, 100\,pm.
    \item IRC via Sella: 200 steps per direction, \texttt{dx=0.1}, \texttt{eta=1e-4},
          \texttt{gamma=0.4}.
    \item RMSD threshold: 0.5\,\AA\ (relaxed from earlier 0.3\,\AA).

          unintended (neither matched).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Results}

    \item \textbf{57--67\% intended rate}, substantially higher than the preliminary
          10-sample result (30\%). The relaxed RMSD threshold and larger sample size
          give a more accurate picture.
    \item \textbf{Intended rate is stable or increases with noise} (57\% at 10\,pm,
          67\% at 50\,pm). This is counterintuitive---noise may help the optimizer
          explore a wider basin and settle on the correct TS pathway, while low-noise
          starts may get trapped in nearby but non-target saddle points.
    \item \textbf{23--33\% unintended} likely includes cases where the TS is real but

          Adopting graph isomorphism (as in the LMHE paper) instead of RMSD would
          provide a more rigorous classification.
    \item \textbf{Comparison to LMHE paper:} Their full Hessian method achieves
          $\sim$44\% intended at 0\,pm on 240 Sella reactions. Our 57--67\% intended
          on 30 T1x samples suggests comparable or better performance, though the
          datasets and validation methods differ.
\end{itemize}

\section{Summary of Findings}
\label{sec:summary}
\label{sec:summary}
%======================================================================

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{Eckart projection is the largest improvement.}
          Level~2 vs Level~0 at 50\,pm: 68\% vs 0\% (300 samples). The projection
          removes TR modes and provides physically meaningful $n_{\mathrm{neg}}$
          counts, which is essential for both convergence checking and the GAD
          direction.

    \item \textbf{$\mathrm{dt}=0.005$ with projection is the best overall configuration.}
          94\% at 10\,pm, 91\% at 50\,pm, 87\% at 100\,pm, 51\% at 200\,pm
          (300 samples, 1000 steps). Dominates at every noise level.

    \item \textbf{70\% convergence plateau} from 10--70\,pm with $\mathrm{dt}=0.01$
          (300 samples). Steps scale linearly with noise.

    \item \textbf{Basin of attraction $\sim$100\,pm.} Zero wrong TS found below
          100\,pm across 172 converged optimizations. First wrong TS at 200\,pm.

    \item \textbf{GAD needs saddle-region starts.} Reactant: 6\%, product: 3\%.
          Midpoint interpolation: 29\%.

    \item \textbf{Adaptive dt hurts.} Eigenvalue-clamped adaptive timestep reduces
          convergence by 10--30\,pp. Clear negative result.

    \item \textbf{Tight clamping is irrelevant.} Default 0.35\,\AA\ cap rarely activates.

    \item \textbf{NR-GAD ping-pong hurts, even with damping.} Undamped: 56.7\% vs
          72.3\% baseline. Best damped ($\alpha=0.1$): 94.7\% at 10\,pm but 77.7\%
          at 50\,pm vs 91.3\%. Pure GAD is strictly better.

    \item \textbf{Convergence ceiling $\sim$96\%.} 3000 steps gains only 1--2\,pp
          over 1000 steps. The remaining $\sim$4\% are structurally hard molecules.

    \item \textbf{IRC: 57--67\% intended} (30 samples, 0.5\,\AA\ threshold).
          Intended rate is stable or increases with noise (57\% at 10\,pm, 67\%
          at 50\,pm). 23--33\% find a valid but unintended TS.
\end{enumerate}
